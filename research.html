Research

From physiological signals to responsive systems.

We develop algorithms, software, and embedded platforms for human-centered neurotechnology, rehabilitation, and assistive interaction.

01 / Neural interfaces

Next-generation neural–machine interfaces

Neurorehabilitation systems demand accurate intent recognition, reliable sensors, and responsive computing. This program advances EMG-based neural–machine interface technology through sensor arrays, spatial features, fault-tolerant processing, and computing platforms capable of real-time, data-intensive operation.

The work integrates research with embedded-systems education and creates pathways for undergraduate, community-college, and K–12 participation.

Supported in part by the NSF CAREER program
Next-generation neural–machine interfaces for EMG-controlled neurorehabilitation.
02 / Rehabilitation

Accessible stroke rehabilitation

Stroke rehabilitation resources are unevenly distributed, particularly in rural and low-resource communities. In collaboration with rehabilitation researchers, this work combines wearable sensing, mobile health, virtual reality, and embedded systems to support quantitative assessment and engaging upper-limb rehabilitation outside specialized facilities.

Selected systems

  • VRehab: a low-cost mobile virtual-reality system for upper-limb rehabilitation.
  • outREACH: wearable and mobile tools intended to reduce geographic barriers to rehabilitation.
03 / Embedded intelligence

Intelligence at the edge

Responsive health and assistive systems cannot always depend on remote computing. We investigate energy-aware and application-specific platforms that process sensor data close to the user, enabling low-latency inference, dependable control, and portable operation.

Past platforms have included mobile CPUs, GPUs, FPGAs, microcontrollers, and heterogeneous edge-computing systems.

04 / Human–machine interaction

Wearable and myoelectric interaction

Our human–machine interface research translates electromyography, motion, and other physiological signals into useful control. Applications have included prosthetic hands, virtual arms, gesture recognition, mobile interfaces, and immersive environments.

The systems serve both as research instruments and as accessible platforms for student design, experimentation, and translation.

Earlier work

Wearable sensing for health and resilience

Earlier lab projects examined wearable monitoring for the relationship between anxiety and stuttering, along with sensor-enabled seismic measurement and post-earthquake rescue. These efforts established a broader foundation in wearable sensing, information fusion, and real-world embedded systems.
